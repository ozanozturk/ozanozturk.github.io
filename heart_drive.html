HEART_DRIVE


GENRE: Narrative Typing Game

PLATFORM: PC

ENGINE: Unity

TEAM: 2 (Current) // 5 (Peak)

ROLE: Producer // Game Designer // Writer

DURATION: In development

Prove your humanity as an android!

• Go on a date with an unassuming human stranger.
• Type and combine available words into sentences however you like.
• Convince your date of your humanity, and win their heart.

Features

ANDROID ON A DATE: You are the secret property of HEART_DRIVE Corp., sent out on a first date with a stranger to prove your ability to blend into the human society. Follow the directives of your admin or ignore them at your discretion. Discover the true reasons behind your creation.

CREATIVE TYPING: Use the available vocabulary build your responses as you like. Type your sentences correctly and fast enough to speak at a convincingly human pace and avoid awkward pauses.

CHOICES, CONSEQUENCES: Do you want to flatter your date or push their patience to limit? The choice is up to you—so are the consequences.

Design Highlights

BRIDGING GAMEPLAY & NARRATIVE

The first time I got a hold of a computer as an eight year old, my first instinct was to type what I wanted to do: “Watch Pokemon”. Typing was the most intuitive way to interact with what I thought was an intelligent machine. The designers of earliest narrative games must have felt the same way considering the prevalence of typing as a gameplay mechanic in the classics of 1970s.

It's no secret that games often struggle bridging gameplay and narrative. Adopting a minimalist approach to design and building the entire premise of HEART_DRIVE’s narrative around typing helped me overcome this common problem.

The protagonist of the game was chosen as an android because the action of typing feels most natural when you are helping a machine formulate a response. The rest of the concept, including first date setting, emerged around this strong marriage of gameplay and narrative.

BOLSTERING PLAYER CREATIVITY

Text adventures traditionally allow the player to type whatever they want and let the text parser filter out invalid commands. While the idea is powerful, this unlimited freedom is in reality governed by a very limited set of verbs. The illusion of freedom dissipates as soon as the game shuts your input down and leaves you disappointed for having mustered your creativity in a blank space.

While designing HEART_DRIVE, I embraced the thought "Constraints breed creativity" to gamify the action of typing. When it is time to speak, your android avatar generates a set of context-sensitive words that can be combined to form responses in any way you like. This limitation of vocabulary bolsters creativity while the ticking timer before your date is tipped off brings excitement to gameplay.

With up to 120 combinations possible in a given situation, HEART_DRIVE gives you a lot to play with. As the sentences you could not think of fast enough on your last run gets stuck in your head, you will be motivated to go back and explore other dialogue paths.

MANAGING STORY CONSEQUENCES

The decision to set the comedic tone of the game had everything to do with managing the scope of the writing task in the project.

HEART_DRIVE makes a grand promise: A believable outcome for all responses you may input. Even games offering much less narrative agency falter at this task. Most games tell dramatic stories where one would expect the consequences of an action to drastically change how the next series events would play out. This makes choices in game narratives so obviously illusionary—after all, it's impossible for a writer to cover every possible story outcome based on hundreds, if not thousands of in-game variables.

HEART_DRIVE, on the other hand, is a situational comedy. Unlike dramas, sitcoms explore consequences to character actions in a safe space. Conflicts arising from character actions are solved in one section or episode of the story. This is not a weakness in storytelling—on the contrary, it is the swift delivery of the punchline that evokes humor. The deliberately obnoxious non-playable character of the game reacts immediately to your input, dropping the punchline before moving onto the next thread of the dialogue—thus achieving a convincing loop of comedic choice & consequence.
